    Section 01

    Foundations

    The Essence standard, in a handful of ideas.

    Foundations

    What is Essence — and why use it?

    What it is

    An OMG standard from the SEMAT initiative (Ivar Jacobson and others) that captures the common ground of any software endeavour as a thinking framework, not a fixed method.

    You get a small language, a kernel of universals every endeavour shares, and practices that plug in to form a complete method.

    Why use it

    Methodology fatigue is real. Big-process projects often produce big documents and small progress. Essence flips this: progress is judged by the health of essential things — alphas — not by the documents you wrote.

    It is lightweight, composable, and travels with you across teams and projects.

    The core move: compose your way of working from practices, and judge progress by alpha state.
    Foundations · Element types

    Six element types — the language

    Everything in Essence — kernel and practices — is built from these six. Colours are consistent across every deck in this set.

    Alpha

    Things you progress

    An essential thing whose health you track — it has ordered states.

    Activity Space

    What must be done

    A space for work the endeavour cannot skip; concrete activities fill it.

    Competency

    Abilities people bring

    Held at five levels: Assists → Applies → Masters → Adapts → Innovates.

    Work Product

    Tangible artifacts

    Evidence of an alpha's progress — a document, model, repo, decision record.

    Role

    Patterns of who does the work

    Hats people wear, not job titles. One person can wear several.

    Pattern

    Reusable rules of thumb

    Biases that shape every decision a practice makes.

    Foundations · The state mechanic

    Progress is a state change

    An alpha reaches its next state — that is progress, not the document you wrote. Every alpha moves through ordered states; each state has a checklist.

    Example · Architecture alpha

    1Envisioned→ 2Outlined→ 3Demonstrated→ 4Usable→ 5Established→ 6Evolving
    A practice's job is to move specific alphas from one state to the next. The whole way of working is just the practices applied in a sensible order until every alpha is where you need it.
    Foundations · The state checklist

    A state's checklist settles the dispute

    When a team disagrees on where it is, the checklist is the referee. The state is reached when every item is genuinely true.

    Architecture → Demonstrated

    • ✔The riskiest parts are proven via spike, reference or increment.
    • ✔The key decisions are validated by evidence.

    Stakeholders → In Agreement

    • ✔The minimal expectations of the stakeholder groups are agreed.
    • ✔The representatives are happy with their involvement.
    • ○The team's input is valued by the representatives.
    • ○Priorities are clear and perspectives balanced.

    The current state is the highest state where all items (and all earlier states) are checked.

    Section 02

    Levels of scale

    Before you apply anything, answer one question: what is the System?

    Levels · Name the System

    The System is the system of interest — and it is fractal

    An enterprise is a system of systems; a solution is a sub-system of the enterprise; a configured product is a sub-system of the solution. The same kernel and practices instantiate at any level.

    Outer level

    Enterprise — System = the enterprise

    Enterprise architecture, the paved road, drivers and standing governance are owned here.

    Middle level

    Solution / Project — System = the solution

    Inherits enterprise paved road, drivers and governance as context; owns this solution's architecture and decisions.

    Inner level

    Component — System = a configured product

    A sub-system of the solution, tracked in its own right when it warrants it.

    What is a System at one level is a sub-system at the level above. The boundary you draw decides what every alpha actually measures.
    Levels · Which practice runs where

    Practices have a natural home level

    None is locked there — but their natural home tells you who runs what, where.

    At the Enterprise level

    • Business Scenarios — set the drivers
    • Enterprise MVA — shape the estate
    • MVG — set decision rights, paved road, guardrails

    ↓ hands down as context

    At the Solution level

    • Service Blueprint — design the specific service
    • MVA — architect within the inherited guardrails

    ↑ feeds evidence & new drivers back up

    The four practices

    Discovery, service design, architecture, governance — composed on the kernel.

    The practices · Overview

    Four practices, four roles in the way of working

    Each plugs into the kernel and is responsible for moving specific alphas. Two sit at the front, two form the architecture-and-governance spine.

    Level · Spans levels
    Business Scenarios · What & what it moves

    Business Scenarios — discover and quantify the need

    A structured way to surface and quantify the real business need behind a request, before anyone commits to a solution. From the TOGAF method; runs at the enterprise / capability level.

    What it is

    • Five elements: problem, environment, outcomes, human actors, system actors.
    • Loop: Premise Formed → Verified → Refined → Released.
    • Run as a facilitated workshop sequence, evolving a working model.

    Alphas it moves

    • Business Scenario (sub-alpha) → Released
    • Opportunity → Value Established
    • Stakeholders → In Agreement
    • Requirements → Bounded
    • Architectural Drivers → Quantified
    Business Scenarios · When to use it

    Use it when the need is unclear or contested

    WhenYou don't really know what business problem you are solving, or stakeholders disagree on it.
    WhenThe drivers are vague, anecdotal or non-measurable.
    WhenA new capability or platform investment is being initiated.
    WhenYou need a defensible link between business outcomes and architectural decisions.
    Relations: sits upstream of everything else. Hands quantified drivers down to MVA. Solutions then take those drivers and use Service Blueprint to design how the specific service is delivered.
    Service Blueprint · What & what it moves

    Service Blueprint — design the end-to-end service

    Visualise one service's delivery from both the customer's and the provider's perspectives. Introduced by Shostack (HBR, 1984); runs at the solution / service level.

    What it is

    • Five layers: customer actions, frontstage, backstage, support processes, physical evidence.
    • Three lines: interaction, visibility, internal interaction.
    • Sliced and progressed like a use case: Scoped → Frontstage Mapped → Blueprinted → Validated → In Operation.

    Alphas it moves

    • Service Blueprint (sub-alpha) → In Operation
    • Requirements → Acceptable
    • Stakeholders → In Agreement
    • System → Architecture Selected (supporting systems specified)
    • Opportunity → Value Established
    Service Blueprint · When to use it

    Use it when a service needs designing or fixing

    WhenDesigning a new service end-to-end (a new customer journey or capability).
    WhenAn existing service is failing — fail points, waits and hand-offs need to be surfaced.
    WhenFrontstage and backstage are out of step and need realigning.
    WhenYou need to surface the supporting-system requirements before architecting.
    Relations: takes drivers from Business Scenarios; hands service requirements and supporting-system needs to MVA and delivery. The customer-journey map is a companion input, not part of this practice.
    MVA · What & what it moves

    Minimum Viable Architecture — architect the minimum, just in time

    The smallest architecture that addresses the significant drivers, evolved continuously. Runs at the level of its System (enterprise or solution).

    What it is

    • Frame drivers · envision · outline · demonstrate the riskiest part first · evolve.
    • The architecture is a small set of significant decisions, recorded as ADRs.
    • A Paved Road (the service chassis) makes the compliant choice the easy one.

    Alphas it moves

    • Architecture → Established / Evolving
    • Architecture Decisions → Validated
    • Architectural Drivers → Sustained
    • Paved Road (sub-alpha) → Pervasive
    • System → Demonstrable
    MVA · When to use it

    Use it when there's a significant decision or technical risk

    WhenA choice is significant, costly to reverse, and has multiple credible options.
    WhenThere is technical risk that needs proving before commitment (a riskiest-first spike).
    WhenA team is wandering off the paved road and a review (not a gate) is the right response.
    WhenThe drivers have changed and the architecture needs adapting.
    Relations: consumes drivers from Business Scenarios; takes service & system needs from Service Blueprint; produces decisions and paved-road updates that MVG governs.
    MVG · What & what it moves

    Minimum Viable Governance — guardrails, not gates

    The lightweight decision rights, guardrails and assurance that keep delivery aligned with intent. Spans levels — enterprise sets, solution operates within.

    What it is

    • Govern by guardrails (the paved road) and review the exceptions, not every change.
    • Decision rights: who decides what at each level.
    • Assurance by evidence — paved-road conformance + architecture-review outcomes.

    Alphas it moves

    • Governance → Effective
    • Way of Working → Foundation Established

    Anti-patterns it replaces

    • Stage gates that block delivery
    • Paperwork governance with no link to outcomes
    • Architecture boards that approve everything in advance
    MVG · When to use it

    Use it to keep control without slowing delivery

    WhenDecisions are constantly escalating or stuck waiting for approval.
    WhenYou need to set enterprise guardrails so solution teams can move fast within them.
    WhenCompliance and risk need to be evidenced without theatre.
    WhenThe current way of working is heavier than it needs to be.
    Relations: sits over the top of everything. Consumes architecture-review outcomes and paved-road signals from MVA; sets the context that solutions operate within.

    Composing · The logical order

    Run them in a logical order — it is a loop, and it nests

    Each stage names the practice that leads it and the alpha states it reaches. It is not a waterfall.

    1

    Discover the need

    Business Scenarios · enterprise

    Run the business-scenario loop to find and quantify the real need; set the drivers handed down.

    2

    Design the service

    Service Blueprint · solution

    Blueprint the end-to-end service from both perspectives; surface the fail points.

    3

    Architect the minimum

    MVA

    Envision, shape, prove the risky part; pave the road; record and validate the decisions.

    4

    Govern throughout

    MVG

    Set decision rights and guardrails; let decisions flow within them; assure by exception.

    5

    Build, run & realise value

    a delivery / run practice — the gap

    Build, test, deploy, operate and measure. These engines are the acknowledged gap in this set.

    Section 05

    The assessment workbook

    Measuring progress by the only thing that doesn't lie — alpha state.

    Workbook · Why measure by alphas

    Documents lie. Alpha states are honest

    A document being "done" tells you very little. The state checklist of an alpha tells you exactly what is true and what isn't — and what to do next.

    Why alphas

    • Outcome-based — measures the thing, not the artifact.
    • Self-evident — the checklist makes status undeniable.
    • Pickable — points straight at the next move (which state, which checklist item).
    • Portable — works at any level of scale.

    What the workbook adds

    • A dashboard rolling up every alpha's current state and progress.
    • A tab per alpha with its state checklists as tickable items.
    • An explainer tab for Levels of Scale so the assessment level is explicit.
    • Formula-driven, zero manual roll-up. Tick a box — the dashboard updates.
    Workbook · Structure

    16 tabs in three groups

    Dashboard

    1 tab — rolls everything up

    KPI tiles, area-grouped table, level chips, data-bar progress, RAG status, links into every alpha tab.

    Levels of Scale

    1 tab — orientation

    Nested levels visual, which practice runs where, and the explicit statement of the level being assessed.

    14 Alpha tabs

    One per alpha

    Branded header, KPI strip, state-grouped checklist with tickable items. Tab colour = area · header colour = owner · chip = level.

    Three dimensions are legible at once: area on the tab strip · owner inside · level in the chip.
    Workbook · How to assess an alpha

    Ticking the checklists moves the state

    How it works

    1. Open the alpha's tab.
    2. Read each state's checklist — top to bottom.
    3. Tick the ✔ column (dropdown) where the statement is genuinely true.
    4. The per-state status turns green when all its items are checked.
    5. The Current State KPI updates — it is the highest contiguous state achieved.

    The state's checklist is the referee. If you can't tick it honestly, you haven't reached that state.

    • KPI tiles — the headline health (overall, complete, items).

    Workbook · Cadence

    Using it in practice

    The workbook earns its place by being run regularly, lightly, and together.

    When to assess

    • Every 2 weeks — sprint review or fortnightly cadence.
    • At every governance review — the dashboard is the agenda.
    • After major events — a release, a re-org, a new driver, a failed audit.

    How to act on it

    • Pick the alpha holding you back — the one with low progress or a stuck state.
    • Look at the next unticked checklist item — that's your next move.
    • Choose the practice activity that addresses it (use the decision guide).
    • If an alpha is Enterprise-inherited and stuck, escalate to the enterprise level.
    Roles: each practice owner maintains their own alpha tabs; the team reviews the dashboard together. Honesty about state is more valuable than green bars.
    Section 06

    Wrap-up

    What to take away, and where to go next.

    Wrap-up · Key takeaways

    If you remember six things…

    1

    Essence is a thinking framework, not a method

    Compose practices on the kernel rather than following one methodology end to end.

    2

    Progress = alpha state change

    Not the document you wrote. The checklist of the next state tells you what to do.

    3

    Name the System first

    Enterprise, solution or component — the level decides what every alpha measures.

    4

    Architect the minimum, pave the road

    Decisions, not documents. Review the exceptions, not every change.

    5

    Govern by guardrails, not gates

    Decide at the last responsible moment; assure by evidence.

    6

    Measure honestly

    The workbook makes progress visible — use the dashboard as your standing agenda.
