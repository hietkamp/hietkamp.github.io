    Service Blueprint
    as an Essence practice

    Visualise the service delivery process from both the customer's and the provider's perspective — customer actions, frontstage and backstage, support processes and physical evidence — across the lines of interaction, visibility and internal interaction. Like a use case, the blueprint is sliced and progressed, and it drives the same alphas.

    Service Design · Shostack 1984Service BlueprintCustomer → Solutionpairs with Business Scenarios & MVALevel · Solution / service

    Where it plugs in

    The Customer area, reaching into Solution — the front of the loop, alongside Business Scenarios.

    What it adds

    1 sub-alpha · 5 activities · 2 work products · 6 roles · 5 patterns · +1 competency (Service Design).

    What it advances

    Like a use case: Requirements → Acceptable, Stakeholders → In Agreement, Opportunity → Value Established, and it specifies the System.

    Depends on

    Business Scenarios for the need; MVA & delivery to build the supporting systems.

    Show

    Service blueprinting was introduced by G. Lynn Shostack (“Designing Services That Deliver”, Harvard Business Review, 1984); the layered/line structure follows common service-design practice (e.g. Nielsen Norman Group). Expressing it as an Essence practice — and the use-case analogy — is a synthesis, not an official standard. It owns one sub-alpha and advances kernel alphas the way a Use-Case 2.0 use case does.
